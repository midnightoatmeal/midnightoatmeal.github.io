Philosophy in The Dark Knight

The Swiss psychiatrist Carl Jung’s idea, “people don’t have ideas; ideas have people,” echoes through Christopher Nolan’s 2008 film The Dark Knight. There are many reasons why the storytelling of this film is highly sophisticated, but one of the more important reasons is that, in it, Nolan plays with ideas as gracefully as a good thinker ought to...

What’s highly interesting about these characters, these villains specifically, are that they are not run-of-the-mill everyday bad people, like the thugs and goons, or even the highly organized mafia portrayed in the film...

Nietzschean ideas are very much present in Nolan’s storytelling. In fact, it is quite clear that the Joker has read Nietzsche. From his opening lines in the film, “Whatever doesn’t kill you, simply makes you stranger,” to the fact that he reduces Harvey Dent, the ambitious and idealist D.A. of Gotham who wants to clean up its streets...

Mentored by Ra’s al Ghul himself, Bruce, on the day of his initiation into the League of Shadows, decides, philosophically, that he won’t turn into a monster by fighting them...

Throughout the film, the Joker is making chilling jokes on humanity’s civility and code...

Martha Nussbaum, an Aristotelian scholar and a moral philosopher, upon reading the Greek tragedies, had concluded that, essentially, only good people can have a tragedy...

So following the Joker’s or the Devil’s logic, if existence only brings misery, why keep on sustaining society? What’s the point of civilization? Why value human life?

The Joker, in his final moral experiment, reveals two evacuation ferries, one carrying civilians and the other prisoners, are rigged to explode at midnight unless one group sacrifices the other...
